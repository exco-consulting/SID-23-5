<font color="#29313b">For the purpose of this example, some assumptions are made:</font><br/><ul>
<li><font color="#29313b">Considering two IP hosts H1 and H2, the measurement system is able to initiate pings from H1.</font></li><li><font color="#29313b">No measurement session is established between H1 and H2.</font></li><li><font color="#29313b">H2 responds to H1 ping requests.</font></li><li><font color="#29313b">The arbitrary sampling choice is to make 100 raw measurements at 1-second intervals, every 15 minutes.</font></li></ul>
<font color="#29313b">Multiple metrics of interest could be derived from the sample batches of 100 values: average, minimum, maximum, different percentiles, etc. Only the Average is illustrated here.</font><br/><font color="#29313b">The following illustration explores the classes describing the metric: definition and business value.</font><br/><font color="#29313b">The metricLevel optional attribute is set to 1 as an example. If metricLevel is used, the convention governing the numbering scheme should be included in the description (i.e., “low numbers mean high importance”, or vice-versa). </font><br/><font color="#29313b">Attributes like metricDefinitionStatus and lastUsed are dynamically modified during the lifetime of the object, by other processes which generate attribute-changing events: modification of the metric definition, usage occurrence of the metric, etc.</font><br/><font color="#29313b">Finally, the MetDefBusinessValue is a business-oriented complement to the technical-oriented MetricDefinition.</font><br/>
